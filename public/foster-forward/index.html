Foster Forward: Healing Trauma and Building Futures for Kentucky's Children

The Child Behind the Statistic

Somewhere in Kentucky tonight, a child who has survived more than any child should is waiting — waiting for a bed, waiting for a foster home, waiting for a therapist who has the time and training to help. She is not waiting because no one cares. She is waiting because the system meant to catch her was never built to hold the weight it now carries.

This is the story of thousands of Kentucky children in foster care. It is a story we can change.

A System Straining Under Its Own Good Intentions

Kentucky's own state auditors have named the problem plainly: the continuum of care does not yet meet the needs of the children the state is responsible for. That is a hard truth. It is also the reason this project exists. Kentucky has too many children needing out of home care (OOHC), not enough caregivers to meet the need, and inadequate services for children needing a safe home. Per the state auditor’s report, 8,700 children needed foster care but there were less than 4,600 approved homes.

Children and adolescents in OOHC developed disproportionately higher rates of chronic health conditions, developmental disabilities, mental health diagnosis, substance misuse and poorer health outcomes. These outcomes are largely related to experiencing higher number of adverse childhood experiences (ACEs), barriers to consistent high quality healthcare and unstable or multiple placements1, 2, 3,4 which can often be potentiated by lack of accessible health information and lack of care coordination between the child welfare agency and healthcare systems. 4,5,6 Additionally, more than 70% of children in foster care have a history of child abuse and/or neglect7 and more than 80% have been exposed to significant levels of violence.8 Understanding the adversities and trauma and the effect of these experiences is integral in the care of children and guiding caregivers through the process.

Across the country, nearly 80% of children who need mental health care never receive it — 7.5 million children left without support they desperately need. In Kentucky, the picture is even more urgent: more than 80% of our counties are federally designated shortage areas for health and behavioral health professionals. That means fewer therapists, longer waitlists, and higher caseloads for the clinicians who remain.

For children in foster care — children who have already endured trauma, instability, and loss — this shortage isn't an abstraction. It shows up as care delivered by inexperienced providers, sessions that stop and start as clinicians turn over, and treatment plans that never quite match the child in front of them. When these children don't get better, the system too often blames the child, or the placement, instead of asking whether the care itself was ever set up to succeed.

The human cost compounds from there. Foster parents and kinship caregivers, doing one of the hardest and most generous things a person can do, watch children they love struggle without real progress — and some, understandably, reach a breaking point. Every family that steps back is one less home for a child who needs one. And when there is nowhere else to go, children are sometimes housed in places never meant for children at all: offices, conference rooms, anywhere but a home.

Promising Opportunities for Innovation

Over the past decade, researchers have developed two powerful models for delivering health and behavioral healthcare more wisely — not by spending more, but by spending smarter.

Model One

Stepped care starts every child with the most accessible intervention, then escalates quickly for those who need more. It has been shown to cut wait times, ease caregiver burden, and achieve outcomes equal to gold-standard trauma treatment — at roughly half the cost.

Model Two

Stratified care does the opposite in one important way: it identifies the children with the greatest needs up front and fast-tracks them straight to intensive treatment, so the most vulnerable children never have to wait in line behind a system that doesn't know yet how much they're hurting.

Both models work. Both have been proven in rigorous trials. What no one yet knows — for children in OOHC care, in a state like Kentucky — is which model, or what blend of the two, helps the most children heal the fastest, at a cost every community can sustain.

That missing answer is exactly what Phase I of this project will deliver.

Our Plan: A Two-Phase Path to Lasting Change

Phase I: A Controlled Launch

In this phase, a continuum of services will be provided within the context of a rigorous study that will place Kentucky's foster children — with full ethical oversight and in true partnership with the state — into a stepped care or stratified care condition based on random assignment and then compared to treatment as usual. We will then assess which approach gets children into treatment faster, keeps them there, produces better outcomes, and does the most good per dollar spent. We will also sit down with the children and families to ask what worked, what didn't, and what they feel they need.

Our Approach to the Controlled Launch: In this randomized controlled trial (RCT), we will recruit participants from primary care, patient-centered care, and community providers. If a tailored assessment confirms that outpatient care is right for them, participants will be randomly placed into either the "stepped care" group or the "stratified care" group.

A

Stepped care group: Children, youth and caregivers start with a digital intervention (Step 1), no matter how severe their symptoms are — as long as outpatient care is appropriate for them. After Step 1, we check how well each person responded. Anyone who hasn't improved enough moves up to Step 2 (group therapy or online Cognitive Behavioral Therapy). If that's still not enough, they move up to Step 3 (intensive one-on-one therapy). We collect data after each step, and anyone who improves enough at a given step stops there — they don't move on to the next one. The idea is to start with the simplest, least costly treatment and only use more intensive (and expensive) treatments for people who need them.

B

Stratified care group: People with complex cases go straight to Step 3. Everyone else starts at Step 1, just like in the other group. This approach front-loads more intensive treatment for people likely to need it — specifically two types of patients: those whose condition is best treated with intensive therapy alone, and those with complex cases. The pilot study will provide us with data to establish these thresholds for the Kentucky population.

C

Treatment-as-usual group: we will compare our two groups (stepped and stratified) to a random sample of clients that received care as usual. We'll use a statistical technique (propensity score matching) to make sure this group is comparable to the other two.

We need about 60 people in each of the three groups to draw reliable conclusions. All study procedures will be approved by the University's Institutional Review Board and the Cabinet for Health and Family Services Review Board and will follow the ethical standards set forth in the Declaration of Helsinki.

Qualitative Study

We will conduct focus groups with a random sample of 20% of the children and/or caregivers in each group to gain deeper insights into the feasibility, acceptability, appropriateness, and utility. These responses will be used to inform program design, and assist in the interpretation of outcomes and next steps.

A Closer Look: Refining Our Approach Through Expert Input

Concurrent with the controlled launch, we will convene a meeting of key stakeholders — including individuals with lived experience, and content and process experts at the local, state, and national levels — to inform the design, delivery, and core components of effective, integrated health and behavioral health care. This meeting will take place within the first nine months of the funding period, shaping both programming and study design while laying the groundwork for key strategic partnerships. Central to this convening will be cultivating a collaborative culture in which participants share ownership of the process through meaningful input and shared decision-making.

Phase II: A Full Launch Based on Lessons Learned

The phase takes what we learned in Phase 1 and builds it into something lasting: a comprehensive, evidence-based system of care for Kentucky's children in out-of-home placement and the caregivers raising them —a real, working model the Cabinet for Health and Family Services can put into practice and other states can learn from. Recognizing the complexity of needs in children in OOHC, the American Academy of Pediatrics and the Healthy Foster Care America proposed integrated care as the gold standard for this population. Integrated care aims to improve outcomes by bringing services together at both the community level as well as at a treatment level for physical and mental health. In a majority of healthcare systems, the delivery of health and behavioral health is often fragmented and complex. “Integrated” acknowledges the complexity of needs and aims to coordinate care to meets those needs. Even with the published guidelines, there is often inconsistent or lack of standardized integrated care models that exist for children in out-of-home care. The phase I study will help identify the most effective service delivery method to guide the delivery of integrated care.

The Foster Forward interventions will be delivered by the University of Kentucky through a partnership with the Division of Pediatric Forensic Medicine and the Center on Trauma and Children in an integrated care model.

Experience and Science Lead the Way

As the flagship institution in the state, UK has been a leader in serving children experiencing child abuse and trauma through identification, intervention, innovation, and research. The Kosair for Kids Center for Safe and Healthy Children and Families opened in March of 2024 to address child maltreatment in the state. The Center on Trauma and Children established in 1999 is the epicenter of translational research and practice on child abuse prevention, intervention and service delivery in the state. CTAC develops and disseminates knowledge, technologies and evidence-based treatments that can attenuate the need for OOHC and creates innovations to address the effects of trauma on children, families, systems and the workforce. Central to its core, CTAC uses implementation science to take what works, and determine how to make it sustainable in a community context.

These partners have over a decade of experience working together, and in 2025, established the Institute on Fostering Resilience. The overall mission of the IFR is to provide a continuum of evidence-based programming to address the needs of children and families involved in OOHC, and to support the systems responsible for serving them. The institute takes evidence to action approach, ensuring that the outcomes most relevant to those involved are privileged, and achieved through its programming. To that end, translation research activities are embedded at each phase of the programming to determine efficacy, effectiveness, utility and to allow for real-time adjustments consistent with the latest quality improvement standards. This Foster Forward proposal will advance and extend the work of the IFR, in a strategic, data-driven manner by providing a continuum of evidence-based programming to address the needs of children and families involved in OOHC, and to support the systems responsible for serving them.

Like the IFR, Foster Forward will be led by Dr. Christina Howard and Dr. Ginny Sprang.

Why This Moment Matters

Kentucky does not lack people who care about its children.

It lacks proof of which path turns that care into results. This project provides that proof — and then acts on it.

A gift to this project is not funding one more program layered onto an overburdened system. It is an investment in finding out, once and for all, how to make every dollar already being spent work harder for every child who needs help. It is a bet on a future where a struggling placement isn't written off as a lost cause but recognized as a child who simply hasn't gotten the right care yet — and then gets it.

Kentucky's children have already shown extraordinary resilience just to survive what brought them into care. They deserve a system built with the same resilience — one that meets them where they are, moves as fast as they need it to, and never gives up on the belief that healing is possible.

With your support, we can build that system. Not someday. Starting now.

Next Steps

Kentucky's children deserve care as diverse as the communities they call home — whether that's a neighborhood foster home offering enriched clinical support, or a small town stretching to meet a child's needs with limited resources. Foster Forward bridges that gap. By partnering with key organizations across the state, we extend care beyond health and behavioral services to the enrichment and support every child needs to thrive. Guided by research and hands-on training, we work hand-in-hand with our communities to deliver individualized, state-of-the-art care — building the resilience that helps Kentucky's children not just survive, but flourish.
