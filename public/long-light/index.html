GAINS for Teens — The Long Light
Gameplay Loop & Zone Map
Internal working spec · July 2026 · concept (Josh) mapped against CTAC Part 1 (Stephanie)

A teen, scared of their first therapy appointment tomorrow, falls asleep over a story and dreams they must climb from darkness to a distant light. The climb is the intervention; they wake ready to go.

1. The gameplay loop

One loop repeats per zone as the creature ascends. Each turn:

  • Discover a glyph → watch the message. A carved glyph, scanned with the phone, plays a ~30-second psychoeducation clip left by a traveler who walked this path before.
  • Take on the challenge. A short activity that reinforces what the message just taught.
  • Earn gear (loot). Completing the challenge yields a piece of gear — ideally the coping idea made tangible — that helps with what’s ahead.
  • Traverse to the next zone. A brief arcade crossing (~30–60s) that uses the gear and carries the creature into the next, brighter environment — where the next glyph waits.

2. Design principles

  • No-fail, always. Challenges and traversals are forgiving and success-guaranteed — retry freely, no game-over, no score-shaming. A therapeutic dose can’t be gated behind dexterity, and “I failed at coping” is a message we must never send.
  • Gear is the skill. Where possible each reward is the coping idea in physical form (a grounding “anchor,” a naming “lantern”). By the summit the creature is fully equipped — which reads as “you’ve built a toolkit for tomorrow.”
  • Understanding brightens the world. The psychoeducation arc is lined up with the dark→light gradient: the confusing, frightening material sits in the low dark zones; understanding and hope arrive as the world lightens.
  • Alone → not alone. The teen sets off resentful and alone; every glyph is proof others walked here first; by the top they’re accompanied — and the caregiver’s “I’ll meet you there” pays off at the summit.
  • The phone is the interface. The real thing they fell asleep holding becomes the in-world tool: scanner, map, inventory (the toolkit), and where the caregiver’s check-ins arrive.
  • One reusable traversal. A single arcade pattern reskinned per zone — low-twitch, one-handed — not five separate mini-games.

3. The frame story

Real-world scenes play in first person — we see the teen’s hands, the book, the phone, the caregiver across the room, but never their face or body. The only character ever seen is the neutral creature they become in the dream, which keeps the whole thing free of gender, race, or body type.

  • Setup (POV). Something happened — kept vague — and the teen has trauma symptoms. The caregiver says there’s an appointment in the morning with a new therapist. The teen is nervous but agrees.
  • Drifting off (POV). To settle their mind they read a favorite story; we see it in their hands; they fall asleep into its world.
  • The call to start. Darkness, a single candle. A text from the caregiver: “I’ll meet you there — just follow the map.” A map of distinct areas appears; irritated at going alone, the teen sets off.
  • The reveal. The camera leaves first person: catching their reflection, the teen sees they’ve become a creature of the story — the first “themselves” they see is neutral. The phone came with them.
  • Messages from those before (the loop begins). Glyphs left by travelers who were here first carry the psychoeducation — peer voices, not lectures.
  • Arrival and waking (POV). At the summit the therapist’s door opens into brilliant light; we cut to first person as the teen wakes — “Time to get ready for our appointment” — ready to go.

4. The messengers

Original creatures of the story’s world — no borrowed IP. Each embodies one of the four trauma-reaction categories, reads as a simple dark silhouette in the vector style, and recovered with support (the peer voice). They do double duty: the travelers you meet at glyphs and the cast of the Character Examples challenge. Clinical wording is Stephanie’s to own; these are the vessels.

  • The Emberwick — reactivity / hypervigilance. Oversized ears and a lantern that flares at every sound; never sleeps. Learned to tell real danger from echoes so its flame could rest.
  • The Mirefly — intrusion. A moth trailed by fading after-images, circling the same flame. Learned the memories were echoes, not the event returning, and they loosened once faced with help.
  • The Hollowshell — avoidance. Curled in a sealed shell, light leaking from one crack. Learned that opening a sliver at a time, with support, let the warmth back in.
  • The Dimmet — negative mood / thoughts. Hunched under its own dark cloud, glow turned low. Learned those thoughts weren’t facts, set the weight down, and turned its light back up.
